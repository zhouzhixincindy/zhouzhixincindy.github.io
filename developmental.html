Penn · Dr. Sharon Wolf
Lab · 01

RIPPLE Lab

Adolescent mental health in Ghana
What I Contributed
Worked with survey data from 2,476 Ghanaian adolescents and parents, the research applies statistical models to create measurement for the Ghana context. Informs a paper in preparation.
Method
Large-scale psychometrics on the full dataset:
  • split-half exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis to adapt the parenting measure
  • latent profile analysis to identify distinct parenting profiles
  • descriptive and demographic analyses linking those profiles to family context
What Surfaced

Western tools do not always travel

A US parenting questionnaire lost its structure when used with Ghanaian teenagers, and needed item-level adaptation.

Meaning shifts across cultures

What "warmth" or "discipline" looks like depends on context. Even tested questionnaires do not automatically carry over.

Poverty shapes parenting

Food insecurity, economic stress, and caregiver distress shape parenting in ways measurement tools have to account for.

Penn · Dr. Xinyin Chen
Lab · 02

Dr. Chen’s Lab

Free-play behavioral coding
What I Contributed
Watched hundreds of hours of video of Chinese 4-year-olds playing in groups, and translated what they did into structured data. Refined the coding system as new patterns showed up, and cross-checked with other coders to make sure we were seeing the same things.
Method
Watched 25-minute play sessions with groups of four children and scored more than 20 behaviors on a 0-2 scale, from individual emotions to group dynamics.

two levels of the coding scheme, 20+ behaviors in total

What Surfaced

Gendered behavior patterns

Girls more often in quiet, cooperative play; boys more often in rough-and-tumble.

Small societies formed fast

Leaders, rule-makers, and followers emerged within minutes. Sometimes, so did exclusion.

No two four-year-olds are alike

Even within one group, kids varied widely in shyness, focus, and how often they jumped in.

Penn · Dr. Michael Nakkula
Lab · 03

Consortium for Mental Health and Optimal Development

Qualitative Coding for School District Partnerships
What I Contributed
Translated a year of reflective memos from district staff into a longitudinal report that a Philadelphia-area school district’s leadership used to inform their strategic plans for the following school year.
Method
All year long, district counselors, teachers, and administrators wrote short reflective memos every two months. I read and coded every one, watched how the themes changed from fall to spring, and turned the whole year into one report the district could act on.
What Surfaced

The analysis traced how district priorities shifted across the year, surfacing three patterns that needed attention.

Role ambiguity Demand-capacity gaps Uneven data access

Project · 02

What I Say, and What I Hold Back

A qualitative study of conflict with parents and friends
What We Argued
The same person handles conflict very differently depending on who they are in conflict with. With parents, hierarchy, filial respect, and cultural expectation shape what gets expressed and what gets held back. With friends, the playing field is more equal, so disagreement can be more direct. For Chinese international students living between two cultural worlds, conflict becomes a quiet place where identity is negotiated.
