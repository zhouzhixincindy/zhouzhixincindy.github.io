“Are children talking to a tool, or to a partner?”

Children today are growing up with AI that talks back. Voice assistants, AI tutors, and chatbots are being introduced into children’s everyday lives, becoming an additional partner and a new social environment. Yet we still lack the knowledge of how this shapes children’s development. My research sits inside this question: what are children actually doing when they talk to AI, and what do those interactions tell us about how to build it well?

Project · 01

AI Perception and Trust

Children's discernment of AI and human voices
What I Contributed
  • Led the design of the experiment platform.
  • Cleaned and coded 10,000+ lines of quantitative behavioral data from 31 children.
  • Refined the task flow, voice stimuli, and questions for participants ages 6 to 10.
Method
A 12-condition experiment that varied voice social cues (emotion, humor, praise, thanking, mental-state talk, theory of mind) to test how children tell AI voices from human ones.
What Surfaced

early pilot, 31 children

Social cues blur the line

With any social cue in the voice, children called it "human" far more often: about 70% of the time, up from 44% with no cue, whether the speaker was AI or a person.

Deeper cues seemed to pull harder

Cues that tap social cognition (theory of mind, mental-state talk) drew more AI-as-human errors than surface cues like emotion. The larger study is built to test it.

Open Questions

Does age change the read?

Whether older children resist the cues, which a larger sample is built to test.

Does knowing AI help?

Whether children with more experience of AI are harder to fool.

Harvard · Child-Centered AI Lab · Dr. Ying Xu
Project · 02

Information Seeking

Children's questions and trust with an AI chatbot
What I Contributed
  • Analyzed 6,000+ child-AI dialogues to map how children ask, explain, and relate.
  • Built and refined the coding scheme, then cross-checked it with other coders.
  • Traced how children's questions shift across the turns of a conversation.
Method
A child-friendly chatbot deployed in 50+ homes for two weeks answered children's own questions and prompted them to judge each answer's trustworthiness.
What Surfaced

They asked far beyond facts

Most questions were factual, but children also asked playful, personal things, of the AI and of themselves.

Curiosity drove the follow-ups

Children chained questions and dug deeper, closer to a real line of inquiry than one-off lookups.

Open Questions

Can asking AI teach?

Whether the questions children chase actually turn into knowledge.

Where does trust break?

When children stop questioning and accept an AI's answer as true, even when it isn't.

Thesis

From Tool to Social Partner

A laddered analysis of children's social engagement with a voice-based chatbot
What I Did
  • Analyzed two weeks of at-home conversations between 52 children (ages 6 to 10) and a voice chatbot.
  • Qualitatively coded children's personal questions into two original ladders of social engagement.
  • Traced step-versus-jump transitions to see how children's engagement moved across turns.
Method
Iterative qualitative coding of 620 personal questions (from 3,277 question-answer pairs) into two parallel ladders, with a step-versus-jump analysis of how children moved between levels.
The Framework
What Surfaced

Social from the start

Children jumped to social levels more than they climbed (110 jumps to 67 steps), as if it were the default.

Nearly everyone got personal

50 of 52 children asked personal questions, and most (79%) were about Curio itself.

They cast Curio in human roles

Children handed Curio roles like rule-setter, coach, confidant, and social mirror, the roles they give a parent or peer.

Open Questions

Will children attach to AI?

Which roles they hand over, and what happens if attachment shifts from caregivers to AI.

Caregiver or competitor?

Whether AI and caregivers complement each other or compete for the same space.

what I’m asking next?
As children blur the line between AI and human voices, will that strengthen engagement and learning, or open a door to blind trust?
How can we design AI educational tools that support healthy skepticism and effective error detection?
Which social roles do children hand over first, and under what conditions? As conversational AI becomes more capable and embodied, will caregivers and AI complement each other, or compete for the same emotional space and authority?
